What is it?

After World War II, Japan's top national priority was to rebuild the economy. What came out of the shift was a work culture where young men, later, women are given lifelong job security in exchange for their loyalty. The salaryman culture promised stability, social acceptance and praises for the workers' unyielding resolve to ultimately contribute to the nation's economic growth through devotion to their employer.

Karoshi, which can be translated literally as "overwork death", is a Japanese term relating to occupational sudden death. The most common causes of karoshi deaths are heart attacks, strokes due to stress, starvation diet but it also includes suicide due to mental fatigue and depression caused by prolonged periods burnouts from a toxic social pressure and working environment.

Karoshi Awareness Timeline

  • 1946

    Japan works to recover from the toll of World Warr 2 on the country. it needed to repair it's economy and rebuild it's infrastructure to be more adaptable and keep up with the rest of the world.

  • 1955

    Japan recovers from the war within 10 years and managed to gain momentum in it's economic growth by the 70s. The traditions of unrelenting devotion and subservience to the lord of the samurai finds a new home within the framework of national growth, from which the *salaryman tradition gains tranction.

  • 1969

    The first fatality related to Karoshi occurred in 1969, when a 29-year-old male employee of a newspaper firm died suddenly at work from a stroke. Over the next few years, there were an increasing number of reports of "sudden occupational fatality, as it was dubbed, until the term "Karoshi" became popular.

  • 1970

    Considerable social concern over health concerns caused by excessive working hourse arose. Some effects noticed were increase of number of people suffering from heart disease and mental instability leading to suicide.

  • 1982

    A book entitled karoshi by Tajiri Seichiro, Hosokawa, and Uehata brought the issue into public view.

  • 1988

    A Karoshi hotline was formed, and in only two years, it received over 1,800 calls.

  • 1990

    During the boom economy and through the 1990s, karoshi became well-known when a number of high-ranking executives died unexpectedly and without warning. Government ministries began to take notice of the issue.

  • 1995

    One in four male employees, worked more than 60 hours per week, according to the Labor Force Survey. A 2.4 fold increase when compared to 1975 figures. Despite the fact that the statistical threshold being 60 hours or more per week, a typical work week might easily be 70-90 hours.

  • 2001

    The Ministry of Health, Labor and Welfare(MHLW) established guidelines for recognizing karoshi in relation to the number of overtime hours worked. These guidelines were developed based on the findings of a literature study and medical examinations that showed a link between excessive work and brain and heart problems.>According to the International Labor Organization(ILO), 28% of Japanese employees worked 50 hours or more per week in 2001.Over 6 million individuals worked 60 hours or more each week, according to official data between the years 2000-2004

  • 2002

    MHLW identified around 30 instances of brain and heart illnesses as labor accidents stemming from overwork increased. These include direct impact such as mental instability and depression, as well as indirect impact like suicide. The MHLW started a program to avoid health damage due to overwork.The term Karoshi is added to the Oxford English dictionary. Health guidelines for overworked workers was implemented as law. The MHLW reported 328 Japanese employees who suffered karoshi.The number is a little over 7 times higher than results recorded in the year 2000.

  • 2014

    Following the death of Dentsu employee Matsuri Takahashi, the government implemented Premium Friday, a program that allows employees to leave at 3pm on the last Friday of each month. However, with monthly finances and sales objectives coming to a close at the end of the month, those that take advantage will just have to work longer hours to compensate for the missed afternoon.

  • 2016

    600,000 Chinese workers die from overworking annually.

    A cover up of an NHK employee who died from overwork after logging 159 hours of overtime in a month is publicized.

  • 2017

    In South Korea, hundreds of employees died from overwork according to official data, forcing the South Korean government to adapt a law cutting the maximum weekly working hours from 68 to 40, with 12 hours of compensated overtime.

  • 2020

    Est.57% of Japanese teachers worked past karoshi threshold in July amid the COVID19 pandemic.

  • 2021 "The "Karoshi" Phenomenon is Now a Worldwide Problem". Working 55 hours or more per week is a serious health hazard according to WHO.

Signs of Burnout


On the Body

  • Lowered Immunity
  • Frequent Headaches or Muscle Pain
  • Change in Appetite
  • Difficulty Sleeping
  • Heart Problems
  • Extreme Fatigue
  • Substance Use
  • Imbalanced Lifestyle

On Mental Health

  • Mind Fog
  • Emotional Numbness
  • Feeling Helpless, Trapped and Defeated
  • Detachment
  • Cynical Outlook
  • Loss of Motivation
  • Lack of Creativity and Purpose
  • Depression
  • Anxiety
  • Suicide Thoughts

On The Workplace

  • Leading to higher rate of errors, social disharmony and disatisfaction on individuals and teams.
  • Low level of overall productivity and high cost of operations, work maintenance, and potentially high rate of errors. Increase of absenteeism and turnover.
  • Disharmony due to work disatisfaction - resulting from a variety of issues such as emotional exhaustion, cynicism, fatigue, as well as low level of yield in productivity

On The Economy

  • According to the World Economic Forum, the costs of turnover and decreased production linked with burnout cost $322 billion each year.
  • It also results in a loss of tax income of $14.6 to $19.5 billion each year.
  • Stanford researchers looked into how workplace stress affects health costs and mortality in the United States (pdf), they found that it led to spending of nearly $190 billion — roughly 8% of national healthcare outlays — and nearly 120,000 deaths each year.

What Can You Do?


Individuals Businesses Government
  • Workers must take a firm position that they will not accept unstated demands to work unreasonably long hours.
  • Individuals need to prioritize healthy work-life balance and be proactive in communicating their boundaries.
  • Develop healthier habits in place of escapism tendencies such as smoking, drinking, gambling and porn.
  • Change has to come from the insie out. Every company, regardless of size, must be aware of the hazards that its employees face and enact strong policies against excessive overtime.
  • Turn out the lights at the end of the day in an effort to keep people from lingering too late.
  • Promote healthy work-life balance.
  • Integrate and promote healthy lifestyle into the office culture.
  • Provide counselling for its employees.
  • Destigmatize discussions surrounding mental issues.
  • Encourage openness between workers regardless of seniority.
  • Encourage workers to use their paid vacations.
  • Stringent rules must be enforced. Many believe that karoshi won't just disappear overnight.
  • Fining companies for not enforcing a strict cap on overtime work.
  • Promote the prioritization of the family over archaic hierarchical values.
  • Promote and encourage self sufficiency in business through incentives for freelances, sole traders, startup oweners and small business owners.
  • Given the slow speed at which cultural attitudes and institutions change, there is a limit to what can be done through regulation.
